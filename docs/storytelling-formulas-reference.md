## 1.1 The Three-Act Structure (Classical Hollywood)

The foundational framework of Western screenwriting. Based on a 120-page screenplay (1 page = ~1 minute of screen time).

### Act I: Setup (Pages 1-30 | 0-25% of runtime)

| Beat | Timing | Runtime % | Purpose |
|------|--------|-----------|---------|
| Opening Image | Page 1 | 0-1% | Visual thesis; establishes tone, mood, genre |
| Exposition/World | Pages 1-10 | 1-8% | Introduce protagonist, world, status quo |
| Inciting Incident | Pages 10-12 | 8-10% | Event that disrupts equilibrium; the story begins |
| Debate/Refusal | Pages 12-25 | 10-21% | Protagonist resists the call; stakes are established |
| Plot Point 1 (First Turning Point) | Pages 25-30 | 21-25% | Irreversible decision; protagonist commits to the journey |

### Act II: Confrontation (Pages 30-90 | 25-75% of runtime)

| Beat | Timing | Runtime % | Purpose |
|------|--------|-----------|---------|
| Act II-A: Rising Action | Pages 30-55 | 25-46% | Protagonist pursues goal; encounters obstacles |
| Pinch Point 1 | Page 45 (~37.5%) | 37-38% | Reminder of antagonist's power; raises stakes |
| Midpoint | Page 55-60 | 46-50% | Major revelation or reversal; false victory or false defeat |
| Act II-B: Complications | Pages 60-75 | 50-63% | Stakes escalate; allies are tested; setbacks mount |
| Pinch Point 2 | Page 75 (~62.5%) | 62-63% | Antagonist's power demonstrated again; linked to Pinch 1 |
| All Is Lost / Crisis | Pages 75-85 | 63-71% | Protagonist's lowest point; seems impossible to win |
| Plot Point 2 (Second Turning Point) | Pages 85-90 | 71-75% | New information or decision propels story toward climax |

### Act III: Resolution (Pages 90-120 | 75-100% of runtime)

| Beat | Timing | Runtime % | Purpose |
|------|--------|-----------|---------|
| Act III Begins | Page 90 | 75% | Protagonist marshals resources for final push |
| Climax Build | Pages 90-110 | 75-92% | Accelerating action toward final confrontation |
| Climax | Pages 110-115 | 92-96% | Final confrontation; theme is proven or disproven |
| Resolution/Denouement | Pages 115-120 | 96-100% | New equilibrium; closing image mirrors opening |

## 1.2 Save the Cat Beat Sheet (Blake Snyder) -- All 15 Beats

Based on a 110-page screenplay model. All page numbers are for a 110-page script; percentages apply universally.

| # | Beat Name | Page(s) | Runtime % | Duration | Description |
|---|-----------|---------|-----------|----------|-------------|
| 1 | **Opening Image** | 1 | 0-1% | 1 page | A visual snapshot of the protagonist's world BEFORE the journey. Sets tone, mood, type, and scope. Should be the thematic opposite of the Final Image. |
| 2 | **Theme Stated** | 5 | ~5% | 1 page | Someone (usually not the protagonist) states the theme of the movie. The protagonist does not yet understand this truth. Often delivered in dialogue. |
| 3 | **Set-Up** | 1-10 | 1-9% | 10 pages | Introduces the protagonist, their world, their flaws ("things that need fixing"), and the supporting cast. Plants every character and situation that will pay off later. The "before" snapshot. Establishes at least 6 things that need fixing. |
| 4 | **Catalyst** | 12 | ~11% | 1-2 pages | The inciting incident. Life-changing event: a telegram, getting fired, meeting someone, a diagnosis. Breaks the status quo. Cannot be a decision by the protagonist -- it must happen TO them. |
| 5 | **Debate** | 12-25 | 11-23% | ~13 pages | The protagonist hesitates. "Should I go? Is this wise?" This is the last chance for doubt. Can include preparation, gathering information, or seeking advice. The question: "What would you do?" |
| 6 | **Break into Two** | 25 | ~23% | 1-2 pages | The protagonist makes a proactive choice to enter Act II. This must be a DECISION, not something forced on them. They leave the "thesis" world and enter the "antithesis" world. |
| 7 | **B Story** | 30 | ~27% | 1-3 pages | Introduction of a new character or relationship (often the love interest). This storyline carries the theme. The B Story provides a breather from the A Story and often is where the theme is explored and discussed. |
| 8 | **Fun and Games** | 30-55 | 27-50% | ~25 pages | The promise of the premise. This is why the audience bought the ticket. The trailer moments. If the movie is about a cop going undercover as a woman, this is where we see the comedy of that situation. The most entertaining section. |
| 9 | **Midpoint** | 55 | ~50% | 1-3 pages | Either a false victory (things seem great but aren't) or a false defeat (things seem terrible but will improve). Raises the stakes. Often mirrors the All Is Lost beat. The clock starts ticking (time stakes introduced). A and B stories cross paths. |
| 10 | **Bad Guys Close In** | 55-75 | 50-68% | ~20 pages | Internal dissent within the team. External pressure from the antagonist. Things fall apart. Bad guys regroup and attack. Internal flaws begin to surface. Allies scatter or betray. Both literal and metaphorical "bad guys" close in. |
| 11 | **All Is Lost** | 75 | ~68% | 1-2 pages | The opposite of the Midpoint. If Midpoint was a false victory, this is a real defeat (and vice versa). Often includes a "whiff of death" -- someone dies, or there's a metaphorical death (end of a relationship, job, dream). The old way of doing things must die. |
| 12 | **Dark Night of the Soul** | 75-85 | 68-77% | ~10 pages | The protagonist's lowest emotional point. Mourning, wallowing, regret. This is the moment before the solution arrives. The darkness before the dawn. The character must process the loss before finding the answer. |
| 13 | **Break into Three** | 85 | ~77% | 1-2 pages | The solution is found. Thanks to characters from the B Story (often), the protagonist discovers the truth. A and B stories merge. The protagonist has the missing piece. Eureka moment driven by the theme. |
| 14 | **Finale** | 85-110 | 77-100% | ~25 pages | The protagonist applies the lesson learned. Implements the plan. Confronts the antagonist. Old world is destroyed, new world is created. The protagonist proves they have changed. Five-point Finale sub-structure: (1) Gathering the team, (2) Executing the plan, (3) High tower surprise (unexpected obstacle), (4) Dig deep down (protagonist uses inner change to overcome), (5) Execution of new plan. |
| 15 | **Final Image** | 110 | 100% | 1 page | The opposite of the Opening Image. Proof that change has occurred. Visual evidence of the protagonist's arc. The "after" snapshot. |

### Save the Cat: 10 Genre Categories (Story Types)

| Genre | Core Pattern | Key Requirement |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|
| Monster in the House | Sin + enclosed space + monster | A transgression creates the monster |
| Golden Fleece | Road/quest + team + prize | Journey transforms the hero |
| Out of the Bottle | Wish + lesson + comeuppance | Careful what you wish for |
| Dude with a Problem | Ordinary person + extraordinary situation | Innocent hero, sudden threat |
| Rites of Passage | Life problem + wrong way to handle it | Growth through pain |
| Buddy Love | Two people + complication + completeness | They complete each other |
| Whydunit | Dark secret + investigation + revelation | Why did they do it? |
| The Fool Triumphant | Underdog + establishment + transformation | Foolishness is wisdom |
| Institutionalized | Group + choice + sacrifice or branding | Individual vs. institution |
| Superhero | Special power + nemesis + curse | Gift is also a burden |

## 1.3 The Hero's Journey (Joseph Campbell / Christopher Vogler)

Based on Campbell's "The Hero with a Thousand Faces" and adapted for screenwriting by Vogler in "The Writer's Journey." Organized into 12 stages across three acts.

### Act I: Departure (0-25% of runtime)

| Stage | Runtime % | Description |
|-------|-----------|-------------|
| **1. Ordinary World** | 0-10% | The hero's normal life before the adventure. Establishes contrast with the Special World. Shows the hero's flaws, desires, and what's at stake. The audience identifies with the hero here. |
| **2. Call to Adventure** | 10-12% | A challenge, quest, or problem is presented. The status quo is disrupted. Can be external (a message, a disaster) or internal (a realization, a dream). |
| **3. Refusal of the Call** | 12-17% | The hero hesitates or outright refuses. Shows fear, inadequacy, or obligation. Raises the stakes -- what will happen if the hero doesn't act? Sometimes a mentor overcomes this refusal. |
| **4. Meeting the Mentor** | 17-22% | The hero encounters a guiding figure who provides training, equipment, or advice. The mentor does NOT accompany the hero the entire way. Represents the highest aspirations of the hero. |
| **5. Crossing the Threshold** | 22-25% | The hero commits to the adventure. Leaves the Ordinary World and enters the Special World. Often a physical crossing (door, border, portal). Point of no return. |

### Act II: Initiation (25-75% of runtime)

| Stage | Runtime % | Description |
|-------|-----------|-------------|
| **6. Tests, Allies, Enemies** | 25-50% | The hero navigates the Special World. Finds allies, confronts enemies, learns the rules. Often set in bars, bases, or training grounds. The hero's character is tested and revealed. |
| **7. Approach to the Inmost Cave** | 45-50% | The hero prepares for the major challenge. The "inmost cave" is the headquarters of the antagonist or the location of the greatest danger. Corresponds roughly to the Midpoint. Planning, reconnaissance, emotional preparation. |
| **8. The Ordeal** | 50-60% | The hero faces their greatest challenge so far. A near-death experience (literal or metaphorical). The central crisis of the story. The hero must "die" so they can be "reborn." Everything the hero has learned is tested. |
| **9. Reward (Seizing the Sword)** | 60-70% | The hero takes possession of what they came for: treasure, knowledge, reconciliation, a special weapon. Celebration. But danger is not over. May include a love scene or moment of clarity. |

### Act III: Return (75-100% of runtime)

| Stage | Runtime % | Description |
|-------|-----------|-------------|
| **10. The Road Back** | 75-85% | The hero must deal with the consequences of confronting the antagonist. Often a chase scene or pursuit. The hero recommits to completing the journey. The ticking clock resumes. |
| **11. Resurrection** | 85-95% | The final and most dangerous confrontation. The hero must use everything learned. A second "death and rebirth" -- the hero is purified. The climax of the story. The theme is proven. The hero is transformed. |
| **12. Return with the Elixir** | 95-100% | The hero returns to the Ordinary World, transformed. Brings back the "elixir" -- treasure, love, wisdom, or freedom. The world is changed. If the hero hasn't learned, they may be doomed to repeat the cycle. |

## 1.4 Dan Harmon's Story Circle (8 Steps)

A simplified, character-centric adaptation of the Hero's Journey. Used extensively in TV episodic writing (Community, Rick and Morty). Each step occupies roughly 12.5% of the story if evenly distributed, but in practice the distribution is variable.

| Step | Position on Circle | Runtime % (Approx) | Description |
|------|--------------------|---------------------|-------------|
| **1. YOU** (Comfort Zone) | Top (12 o'clock) | 0-5% | Establish the character in their zone of comfort. This is who they are. The status quo. |
| **2. NEED** (Desire) | Upper right (1:30) | 5-12% | The character wants something. A desire, a lack, a problem. Something is missing. This creates forward momentum. |
| **3. GO** (Unfamiliar Situation) | Right (3 o'clock) | 12-25% | The character enters an unfamiliar situation. Crosses the threshold. This is the point of no return. |
| **4. SEARCH** (Adaptation) | Lower right (4:30) | 25-50% | The character adapts to the new situation. They struggle, learn, meet allies and enemies. The "road of trials." This is the longest section. |
| **5. FIND** (Getting What They Wanted) | Bottom (6 o'clock) | 50-55% | The character gets what they wanted -- or thinks they do. The midpoint. Often a false victory or the achievement feels hollow. |
| **6. TAKE** (Paying the Price) | Lower left (7:30) | 55-75% | There is a heavy price for getting what they wanted. Consequences, sacrifices, complications. The "bad guys close in" territory. Things get worse. |
| **7. RETURN** (Going Back) | Left (9 o'clock) | 75-90% | The character returns to their familiar situation. But they are not the same. They must confront the final challenge using what they learned. |
| **8. CHANGE** (Transformation) | Upper left (10:30) | 90-100% | The character has changed. They are capable of change. The new status quo. The character has either grown or revealed their true nature. |

### Story Circle: Upper Half vs. Lower Half

- **Top half (Steps 1-2, 7-8)**: ORDER -- the known world, comfort, civilization, consciousness
- **Bottom half (Steps 3-4, 5-6)**: CHAOS -- the unknown, the unconscious, the special world, danger

### Story Circle: Left Half vs. Right Half

- **Left half (Steps 1, 8, 7, 6)**: The character themselves -- identity, self, internal
- **Right half (Steps 2, 3, 4, 5)**: The external world -- goals, other people, environment

## 1.5 Syd Field's Paradigm

The original codification of screenplay structure from "Screenplay: The Foundations of Screenwriting" (1979). Based on a 120-page screenplay.

### Core Structure

```
ACT I (Setup)     |  ACT II (Confrontation)              |  ACT III (Resolution)
Pages 1-30        |  Pages 30-90                          |  Pages 90-120
25% of script     |  50% of script                       |  25% of script
                  |                                       |
     PP1 -------->|<---- Midpoint ---->                   |<-------- PP2
     (p.25-27)    |      (p.60)                           |  (p.85-90)
```

### Detailed Breakdown

| Element | Page | Runtime % | Description |
|---------|------|-----------|-------------|
| **Setup** | 1-10 | 0-8% | Introduce protagonist, dramatic premise, dramatic situation |
| **Inciting Incident** | ~10 | ~8% | Event that sets the story in motion |
| **Plot Point 1** | 25-27 | 21-23% | End of Act I; swings the action in a new direction; a reversal |
| **Pinch 1** | ~45 | ~37.5% | Midpoint of Act IIA; a reminder of the central conflict |
| **Midpoint** | ~60 | ~50% | The key structural element Field added later; a reversal that splits Act II in half |
| **Pinch 2** | ~75 | ~62.5% | Midpoint of Act IIB; linked to Pinch 1; reminds audience of central conflict |
| **Plot Point 2** | 85-90 | 71-75% | End of Act II; swings into resolution; protagonist has what they need |
| **Climax** | ~115 | ~96% | The peak of the dramatic action; the resolution of the main conflict |
| **Resolution** | 115-120 | 96-100% | The new normal; final image |

### Field's Key Principles

1. **A screenplay is structure.** Structure is the foundation of all screenwriting.
2. **Every scene must advance the plot or reveal character.** Preferably both.
3. **The first 10 pages must grab the reader.** Set up the dramatic premise.
4. **Plot points are the structural anchors.** They "hook" the action and spin it in a new direction.
5. **The midpoint connects the first half to the second half.** It's the "link in the chain."
6. **Know your ending before you begin.** The ending determines the beginning.

## 1.6 The Sequence Approach (Frank Daniel / Paul Joseph Gulino)

Divides a feature film into 8 sequences, each 10-15 minutes long. Each sequence has its own internal tension, mini-goal, and resolution. Originated at Columbia University and USC film schools.

### Complete 8-Sequence Breakdown

| Seq | Act | Pages | Runtime % | Title | Description |
|-----|-----|-------|-----------|-------|-------------|
| **1** | I | 1-15 | 0-12.5% | **Point of Attack / Setup** | Hook the audience immediately. Introduce the protagonist and their world. Establish the status quo. End with or build toward the inciting incident. The sequence has its own mini-tension: "Who is this person and what is their world like?" |
| **2** | I | 15-30 | 12.5-25% | **Predicament / Lock-In** | The protagonist responds to the inciting incident. Tries to restore status quo and fails. Situation worsens. By the end of this sequence, the protagonist is locked into the main dramatic question of the film. End of Act I. |
| **3** | II | 30-45 | 25-37.5% | **First Obstacle / Attempt** | The protagonist's first real attempt to solve the problem. New allies, new rules, new world. Initial progress but growing complications. Ends with a minor setback or escalation. |
| **4** | II | 45-60 | 37.5-50% | **Complications / Midpoint** | Stakes rise significantly. The protagonist's approach is not working. Culminates in the Midpoint -- a major reversal, revelation, or shift that changes the protagonist's understanding of the problem. |
| **5** | II | 60-75 | 50-62.5% | **Subplot Rising / New Approach** | Post-midpoint response. The protagonist adjusts strategy. Subplots gain prominence. New obstacles emerge. B-story and A-story begin to intertwine more deeply. |
| **6** | II | 75-90 | 62.5-75% | **Main Culmination / Crisis** | The protagonist's revised plan collides with the antagonist's counter-moves. The crisis point. All Is Lost moment. End of Act II. The protagonist must make a crucial decision. |
| **7** | III | 90-105 | 75-87.5% | **New Tension / Third Act Twist** | The apparent resolution of the central dramatic question shows its problems. New information or obstacles emerge. Stakes reach their highest point. The "third act twist" -- what seemed like the solution creates new danger. |
| **8** | III | 105-120 | 87.5-100% | **Resolution** | The climax and resolution. The central dramatic question is answered. Subplots are resolved. The tension created by the inciting incident is truly resolved. New equilibrium established. |

### Key Principles of the Sequence Approach

1. **Each sequence is a mini-movie.** It has its own setup, conflict, and resolution.
2. **Each sequence has its own dramatic question.** Different from the film's overall question.
3. **The resolution of one sequence creates the tension of the next.**
4. **Somebody wants something badly and is having difficulty getting it.** (Frank Daniel's definition of drama)
5. **Two kinds of stories: chases and escapes.** Every sequence is one or the other.

## 1.7 Pixar's Storytelling Framework

### The Story Spine (Pixar's Core Formula)

```
Once upon a time, there was ___.
Every day, ___.
One day, ___.
Because of that, ___.
Because of that, ___.
Because of that, ___.
Until finally, ___.
And ever since that day, ___.
The moral of the story is ___.
```

### Pixar's 22 Rules of Storytelling (Emma Coats)

1. You admire a character for TRYING more than for their successes.
2. Keep in mind what's interesting to the AUDIENCE, not what's fun for you as a writer.
3. Trying for theme is important, but you won't see what the story is actually about until you reach the end. Then rewrite.
4. Use the Story Spine (above).
5. Simplify. Focus. Combine characters. Hop over detours. You'll feel like you're losing valuable stuff, but it sets you free.
6. What is your character good at, comfortable with? Throw the polar opposite at them. Challenge them. How do they deal?
7. Come up with your ending before you figure out your middle. Endings are hard -- get yours working up front.
8. Finish your story, let go even if it's not perfect. In an ideal world you have both, but move on. Do better next time.
9. When you're stuck, make a list of what WOULDN'T happen next. Lots of times the material to get you unstuck will show up.
10. Pull apart the stories you like. What you like in them is a part of you; recognize it before you can use it.
11. Putting it on paper lets you start fixing it. If it stays in your head, a perfect idea, you'll never share it with anyone.
12. Discount the 1st thing that comes to mind. And the 2nd, 3rd, 4th, 5th -- get the obvious out of the way. Surprise yourself.
13. Give your characters opinions. Passive/malleable might seem likable to you as you write, but it's poison to the audience.
14. Why must you tell THIS story? What's the belief burning within you that your story feeds off of? That's the heart of it.
15. If you were your character, in this situation, how would you feel? Honesty lends credibility to unbelievable situations.
16. What are the stakes? Give us reason to root for the character. What happens if they don't succeed? Stack the odds against them.
17. No work is ever wasted. If it's not working, let go and move on -- it'll come back around to be useful later.
18. You have to know yourself: the difference between doing your best and fussing. Story is testing, not refining.
19. Coincidences to get characters INTO trouble are great; coincidences to get them OUT of it are cheating.
20. Exercise: take the building blocks of a movie you dislike. How would you rearrange them to make something you DO like?
21. You have to identify with your situation/characters -- you can't just write "cool." What would make YOU act that way?
22. What's the essence of your story? Most economical telling of it? If you know that, you can build out from there.

### Pixar's Internal Story Structure (Derived from Their Films)

| Beat | Typical Position | Description |
|------|-----------------|-------------|
| World Establishment | 0-8% | The rules of the world (toys are alive, monsters scare kids, etc.) |
| Character in Context | 8-12% | Who is the protagonist within this world? What do they want? |
| Disruption of Comfort | 12-15% | Something forces the protagonist out of their routine |
| False Solution | 15-25% | The protagonist thinks they know how to fix things |
| Buddy/Companion Introduction | 20-30% | A companion (often initially unwanted) joins the journey |
| Fun & Discovery | 30-50% | Exploring the premise; the "promise of the premise" |
| Midpoint Revelation | 50% | The truth about the situation becomes clear |
| Relationship Deepens | 50-65% | The companion relationship becomes genuine |
| Betrayal/Separation | 65-75% | The companions are separated or trust is broken |
| All Is Lost | 75% | The protagonist realizes their fatal flaw |
| Emotional Climax | 85-95% | The internal transformation (often more important than external) |
| External Climax | 90-95% | The action climax driven by the emotional change |
| New Normal | 95-100% | The world is the same, but the protagonist sees it differently |

## 1.8 Additional Professional Frameworks

### The Mini-Movie Method (Chris Soth)

Divides a film into 8 "mini-movies" of approximately 12-15 minutes each. Similar to the Sequence Approach but emphasizes that each mini-movie has:
- Its own protagonist goal
- Its own antagonist/obstacle
- Its own beginning, middle, and end
- A cliffhanger or transition to the next mini-movie

### The Nutshell Technique (Jill Chamberlain)

Every story needs 8 elements in exact order:

| # | Element | Description |
|---|---------|-------------|
| 1 | SET-UP | Protagonist's flaw-based status quo |
| 2 | POINT OF NO RETURN | Protagonist makes a decision that reveals their flaw |
| 3 | CRISIS | Direct consequence of the flawed decision |
| 4 | CLIMAX | The protagonist must face their flaw head-on |
| 5 | CATCH | What the protagonist wants (external goal) |
| 6 | WANT | What the protagonist wants vs. what they need (lie vs. truth) |
| 7 | FLAW | The specific character flaw driving the story |
| 8 | CRISIS EMOTION | The emotional state at the all-is-lost moment |

## 2.1 Cold Open Structure

### Definition
A cold open (also: teaser, pre-credits sequence, or "cold opening") is a scene or sequence that runs before the opening title sequence or credits.

### Cold Open Types

| Type | Duration | Description | Example Shows |
|------|----------|-------------|---------------|
| **Hook Cold Open** | 30 sec - 2 min | Drops audience into action mid-scene | Breaking Bad, 24 |
| **Mystery Cold Open** | 1-3 min | Presents a puzzle to be solved this episode | Law & Order, CSI |
| **Flash-Forward Cold Open** | 1-5 min | Shows a moment from later in the episode (or season) | Breaking Bad, How to Get Away with Murder |
| **Standalone Comedy Cold Open** | 1-4 min | Self-contained comedic bit, often unrelated to A-plot | The Office, Brooklyn Nine-Nine, Saturday Night Live |
| **Previously On...** | 30 sec - 1 min | Recap of relevant past events | Lost, Game of Thrones |
| **Cold Open + Previously On** | 1-3 min total | Cold open scene followed by recap, then titles | Many network dramas |
| **Extended Cold Open** | 5-15+ min | Streaming/premium shows may delay titles significantly | Stranger Things, The Bear |

### Cold Open Internal Structure

```
1. HOOK (first 5-10 seconds): Immediate visual/audio grab
2. ESTABLISH (next 15-30 seconds): Where are we? Who is present?
3. DEVELOP (30 sec - 2 min): The situation unfolds
4. CLIFFHANGER or PUNCHLINE (final moment): End on question, shock, or laugh
5. TITLE SEQUENCE (or SMASH CUT TO TITLE CARD)
```

## 2.2 A-Plot / B-Plot / C-Plot Structure

### Definitions

| Plot Layer | Screen Time | Purpose |
|------------|-------------|---------|
| **A-Plot** (Main Plot) | 50-60% of episode | The primary storyline; drives the episode; protagonist's main challenge |
| **B-Plot** (Subplot) | 25-35% of episode | Secondary storyline; often carries the theme; provides contrast to A-Plot |
| **C-Plot** (Runner) | 10-15% of episode | Tertiary storyline; often comedic relief; the "runner" or "tag" story |
| **D-Plot** (Thread) | 0-5% of episode | Optional; minor thread; may plant seeds for future episodes |

### Interweaving Patterns

**Standard Weave Pattern (for a 5-act drama):**
```
Cold Open:  A-Plot setup
Act 1:      A-Plot development -> B-Plot introduction -> A-Plot complication
Act 2:      B-Plot development -> A-Plot escalation -> C-Plot introduction
Act 3:      A-Plot midpoint -> B-Plot complication -> C-Plot development
Act 4:      A-Plot crisis -> B-Plot climax -> A-Plot pre-climax
Act 5:      A-Plot climax -> C-Plot resolution -> B-Plot resolution -> A-Plot resolution
Tag:        C-Plot or B-Plot button (comedic/emotional capper)
```

**Thematic Mirroring:** B-Plot should thematically mirror the A-Plot. If A-Plot is about trust, B-Plot explores trust in a different context.

## 2.3 Act Structure: 22-Minute Sitcom (Half-Hour Comedy)

Based on 22 minutes of content (for network with commercials). Multi-cam and single-cam have different conventions.

### Multi-Camera Sitcom (Studio Audience)

| Segment | Duration | Pages | Description |

## 2.6 Season Arc Structure

### The Season as a Three-Act Structure

For a standard 10-13 episode season:

| Act | Episodes | % of Season | Purpose |
|-----|----------|-------------|---------|
| **Season Act I** | Eps 1-3 (of 10) or 1-4 (of 13) | 0-30% | Establish season premise; introduce season antagonist; set up character arcs; define the season question |
| **Season Act II** | Eps 4-7 (of 10) or 5-9 (of 13) | 30-70% | Escalate the central conflict; deepen character arcs; midseason twist (usually ep 5 or 7); subplots develop |
| **Season Act III** | Eps 8-10 (of 10) or 10-13 (of 13) | 70-100% | Rush toward season climax; resolve character arcs; answer the season question; set up next season |

### 22-24 Episode Season (Network)

| Phase | Episodes | Purpose |
|-------|----------|---------|
| **Premiere Block** | 1-3 | Set the season; establish new status quo |
| **Early Season** | 4-8 | Build world, deepen characters; standalone episodes with arc threads |
| **Fall Finale** | 9-11 | Midseason climax; major cliffhanger before hiatus |
| **Winter Return** | 12-14 | Reset after cliffhanger; new complications |
| **Mid-Season** | 15-18 | Deepest character development; may include a "bottle episode" |
| **Pre-Finale Arc** | 19-21 | Accelerate toward finale; dominos start falling |
| **Season Finale** | 22-24 | Season climax; resolve major arcs; cliffhanger for next season |

### Season Arc: Key Structural Beats

| Beat | Typical Position | Description |
|------|-----------------|-------------|
| Season Inciting Incident | Episode 1 (or end of previous season) | The event that defines this season's central conflict |
| Season Question Crystallized | Episode 2-3 | The audience clearly understands what this season is about |
| First Major Setback | Episode 3-4 (~25%) | The initial approach fails |
| Midseason Twist | Episode 5-6 (of 10) or 11-12 (of 22) (~50%) | A major revelation that reframes everything |
| False Victory or False Defeat | Episode 7 (of 10) or 16-17 (of 22) (~65%) | Things seem resolved but aren't |
| Season "All Is Lost" | Episode 8-9 (of 10) or 20-21 (of 22) (~80%) | The lowest point; seems impossible |
| Season Climax | Penultimate or final episode | The season's central conflict reaches its peak |
| Season Resolution / Cliffhanger | Final episode | Resolve this season; tease the next |

## 2.7 Cliffhanger Formulas

### Types of Cliffhangers

| Type | Description | Best For |
|------|-------------|----------|
| **Physical Peril** | Character in mortal danger | Action, thriller, adventure |
| **Revelation** | Shocking information revealed | Mystery, drama, soap opera |
| **Decision Point** | Character must make an impossible choice | Drama, romance |
| **Reversal** | Everything the audience believed is wrong | Mystery, sci-fi, thriller |
| **Arrival/Departure** | Someone unexpected arrives or a key character leaves | Drama, soap opera |
| **Failure** | The protagonist's plan catastrophically fails | Any genre |
| **Moral Dilemma** | Character must choose between two terrible options | Drama, thriller |
| **Time Bomb** | A countdown is revealed; disaster approaches | Thriller, action |
| **Betrayal** | A trusted ally is revealed as enemy | Any genre |
| **Death/Near-Death** | A character appears to die (may or may not) | Any genre |

### Cliffhanger Placement Rules

1. **Every act break should have a mini-cliffhanger** (for network TV with commercials)
2. **Episode cliffhangers should escalate** through the season
3. **Midseason cliffhangers** should be the biggest mid-season surprise, second only to the finale
4. **Season finale cliffhangers** should reframe the entire show's premise
5. **The question the cliffhanger raises must be answerable** -- don't create mysteries you can't resolve
6. **The best cliffhangers are both surprising AND inevitable** -- they feel shocking yet logical in retrospect

### Cliffhanger Escalation Through a Season

```
Episode 1-3:   Mild hooks (curiosity-based)        -> "What will happen?"
Episode 4-6:   Moderate suspense (stake-based)      -> "How will they survive this?"
Episode 7-9:   High tension (betrayal/revelation)   -> "Everything I believed was wrong!"
Episode 10+:   Maximum impact (existential threat)  -> "Can anyone survive this?"
```

## 2.8 Episodic vs. Serialized Structure

### Monster of the Week (Procedural/Episodic)

| Element | Per Episode | Description |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| **Self-Contained Story** | 80-90% of episode | Each episode has a complete beginning, middle, end |
| **Serialized Thread** | 10-20% of episode | Ongoing character arcs, mythology, season arc |
| **Reset Factor** | High | Status quo largely restored each week |
| **Entry Point** | Any episode | New viewers can start watching at any point |

**Structure per episode:**
```
Cold Open: New case/monster/problem presented
Act 1:     Team begins investigation; case details revealed
Act 2:     First theory fails; complication; personal B-story
Act 3:     New evidence; red herring; approaching solution
Act 4:     Confrontation with "monster"; twist
Act 5:     Resolution of case; brief serialized scene (character growth, mythology)
Tag:       Character moment or setup for next episode
```

### Fully Serialized

| Element | Per Episode | Description |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| **Serialized Story** | 90-100% of episode | Each episode is a chapter in a larger story |
| **Self-Contained Beats** | 0-10% | Very few standalone moments |
| **Reset Factor** | None | Every episode permanently changes the status quo |
| **Entry Point** | Episode 1 only | Must watch from the beginning |

### Hybrid (Modern Standard)

Most prestige TV uses a hybrid approach:
- **70% serialized** (season-long arcs advance significantly each episode)
- **30% episodic** (each episode has its own thematic question or mini-quest)
- **Each episode has a clear "episode story"** that also advances the season arc

## 3.1 Comedy

### Setup-Punchline Timing

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Setup** | Establishes the normal expectation (the "straight line"). Typically 1-3 sentences or one visual beat. |
| **Misdirection** | Leads the audience to expect a logical conclusion. The audience's brain fills in the "obvious" answer. |
| **Punchline** | Subverts the expectation. The surprise creates the laugh. Delivered in the fewest possible words. |
| **Timing Gap** | The pause between setup and punchline. In film: typically 1-3 seconds. Too short = audience can't process. Too long = tension dissipates. |

### Rule of Threes

```
Pattern:  Normal, Normal, SUBVERSION
Example:  "I need to bring three things: water, sunscreen, and a priest."

Beat 1 (Establishes pattern):     Expected item/behavior
Beat 2 (Reinforces pattern):      Another expected item/behavior
Beat 3 (Breaks pattern):          Unexpected/absurd item/behavior -> LAUGH
```

**Timing in film:** Each beat typically gets 2-5 seconds. The third beat gets the shortest delivery for maximum surprise.

### Callback Structure

| Stage | Timing | Description |
|-------|--------|-------------|
| **Original Joke** | Early in the story (Act 1) | The joke plays for the first time. Gets a normal laugh. |
| **First Callback** | Middle of the story (Act 2) | Reference to the original joke in a new context. Gets a bigger laugh because of recognition. |
| **Final Callback** | Near the end (Act 3) | The joke returns in the most unexpected or elevated context. Gets the biggest laugh. Often transforms the joke's meaning. |
| **Optional: Tag Callback** | Very end / credits | One last unexpected reference. Gets a laugh of pure delight. |

**Spacing Rule:** Callbacks should be spaced far enough apart that the audience has "forgotten" the joke but close enough that they still remember when triggered. Minimum 5-10 minutes between callbacks in a feature film.

### Running Gags

- **Introduction:** Establish the gag clearly the first time (plays as a normal joke).
- **Repetition 1:** Same gag recurs; audience recognizes it and it's funnier through recognition.
- **Repetition 2:** The gag escalates or appears in a surprising context.
- **Repetition 3+:** Each recurrence should either escalate or subvert the expectation of the gag itself.
- **Final Payoff:** The running gag either reaches its ultimate extreme or is subverted entirely.

**Diminishing Returns Rule:** A running gag that simply repeats without variation will get less funny each time. Each recurrence MUST add something: escalation, new context, subversion, or combination with another joke.

### Comedy Scene Structure

```
1. STRAIGHT MAN sets up the situation (normal)
2. COMIC CHARACTER reacts abnormally (comedic premise established)
3. ESCALATION: the abnormal behavior compounds (3-5 escalation beats)
4. PEAK: the situation reaches maximum absurdity
5. DEFLATION or TOPPER: either a sudden return to normalcy (deflation) or one final escalation that's even more extreme (topper)
```

## 3.2 Drama

### Emotional Escalation Pattern

| Phase | Runtime % | Emotional Level | Description |
|-------|-----------|----------------|-------------|
| **Baseline** | 0-10% | Low-Medium | Establish emotional normal; hint at underlying tension |
| **First Crack** | 10-15% | Medium | The inciting incident creates the first emotional disruption |
| **Slow Build** | 15-35% | Medium-Rising | Tension accumulates through obstacles and complications |
| **First Peak** | 35-40% | High | An emotional confrontation or revelation; partial release |
| **False Calm** | 40-45% | Medium (deceptive) | Things seem to settle; but something is wrong beneath the surface |
| **Intensification** | 45-65% | Rising Sharply | Each scene is more emotionally charged than the last |
| **Second Peak** | 65-70% | Very High | A major emotional confrontation; often involves tears, anger, or confession |
| **Devastation** | 70-80% | Crash | The emotional aftermath; the All Is Lost equivalent |
| **Dark Night** | 80-85% | Very Low | The deepest emotional pain; silence, isolation, despair |
| **Final Build** | 85-95% | Rising to Maximum | The final confrontation; everything on the line |
| **Climax** | 95-98% | Peak | The most emotionally intense moment in the film |
| **Release** | 98-100% | Resolution | Catharsis; tears of relief/joy/sadness; new emotional equilibrium |

### Drama: The Pressure Cooker Model

```
1. Put characters you love in a confined situation
2. Turn up the heat (add pressure gradually)
3. Remove escape routes one by one
4. Force characters to confront what they've been avoiding
5. The pressure forces a transformation (or destruction)
```

### Dramatic Irony Structure

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Setup** | Audience knows something the character does not |
| **Sustained Tension** | Every scene where the character acts in ignorance creates dread/pity |
| **Near-Discovery** | The character almost learns the truth but doesn't (increases tension) |
| **Revelation** | The character finally learns the truth (catharsis or devastation) |
| **Aftermath** | The emotional consequences of the revelation |

## 3.3 Horror / Thriller

### Tension-Release Cycle

```
TENSION CURVE:
    /\      /\  /\    /\    /\  /\  /\
   /  \    /  \/  \  /  \  /  \/  \/  \    /\  CLIMAX
  /    \  /        \/    \/            \  /  \
 /      \/                              \/    \___
 |  Setup |  Building  | Escalating |  Peak  |Res|

Each peak should be HIGHER than the previous one.
Each valley should be SHALLOWER than the previous one.
```

### Horror Beat Pattern

| Beat | Runtime % | Description |
|------|-----------|-------------|
| **Normalcy Established** | 0-8% | Show ordinary life; make audience care about characters |
| **First Unease** | 8-12% | Something feels "off"; subtle wrongness; could be explained away |
| **Inciting Horror** | 12-15% | The first undeniable encounter with the threat |
| **Investigation/Denial** | 15-25% | Characters try to rationalize or investigate; audience knows more |
| **First Attack** | 25-30% | The threat becomes physical/real; first victim or near-miss |
| **False Safety** | 30-35% | A moment of relief; characters regroup; audience exhales |
| **Rules Discovery** | 35-45% | Characters learn how the threat works; hope emerges |
| **Escalating Attacks** | 45-65% | The threat adapts; the rules don't fully work; body count rises |
| **Midpoint Revelation** | 50% | The true nature of the threat is revealed (or what characters thought was true is wrong) |
| **Isolation** | 65-75% | Characters are separated, trapped, or stripped of resources |
| **All Is Lost** | 75-80% | The threat seems unstoppable; key ally is killed or incapacitated |
| **Final Girl/Boy Stand** | 80-90% | The protagonist must face the threat alone with newfound knowledge |
| **Climactic Confrontation** | 90-95% | Maximum terror; the final battle or escape |
| **False Resolution** | 95% | It seems to be over (the monster appears dead) |
| **Final Scare** | 96-98% | One last shock (the monster isn't dead / the threat persists) |
| **True Resolution** | 98-100% | Safety or ambiguity; the new normal (may include sequel-bait) |

### Jump Scare Architecture

| Element | Duration | Description |
|---------|----------|-------------|
| **Build** | 10-30 seconds | Slow approach; quiet soundtrack; character moving through space |
| **False Scare** | 1-2 seconds | A cat, a friend, a falling object -- harmless but startling |
| **Lull** | 3-10 seconds | Audience relaxes ("it was nothing"); guards drop |
| **True Scare** | 1 second | The real threat appears; maximum volume; rapid visual change |
| **Aftermath** | 5-15 seconds | Reaction; running; consequences of the scare |

**Jump Scare Placement Rules:**
- Never use more than 5-7 major jump scares in a 90-minute film
- Space them at least 8-12 minutes apart
- Alternate between false scares and real scares (audiences learn to predict)
- The first jump scare should come between 15-25 minutes in
- The most effective jump scare is at the 70-80% mark (when tension is highest)
- Never put a jump scare in the final 30 seconds of the film (diminishes the ending)

### Thriller: Suspense vs. Surprise (Hitchcock Model)

| Technique | Setup | Duration | Effect |
|-----------|-------|----------|--------|
| **Surprise** | Audience knows nothing; bomb explodes | 15 seconds of shock | Brief jolt; quickly forgotten |
| **Suspense** | Audience sees the bomb under the table; characters don't know | 15 minutes of dread | Prolonged tension; deeply memorable |

**Hitchcock's Rule:** Always choose suspense over surprise. Let the audience know the danger exists. The tension comes from waiting.

## 3.4 Romance

### Romance Beat Sheet

| Beat | Runtime % | Description |
|------|-----------|-------------|
| **Character Introductions (Separate)** | 0-8% | Meet each love interest separately; show their flaws, desires, and incomplete lives |
| **The Meet-Cute** | 8-12% | The first meeting. Must be memorable, often humorous or charged. Creates a strong first impression (positive or negative). |
| **First Spark** | 12-18% | Chemistry is evident to the audience, even if characters resist. Banter, glances, accidental touches. |
| **First Date / Connection** | 18-25% | Characters spend real time together; walls begin to lower; vulnerability emerges |
| **Deepening Bond** | 25-40% | Montage territory. Shared experiences, growing intimacy, falling in love. B-Story complications emerge. |
| **First Obstacle** | 40-45% | External or internal obstacle threatens the relationship. Misunderstanding, rival, career conflict, secret revealed. |
| **Midpoint Commitment** | 45-50% | Despite the obstacle, the couple recommits. Often the first "I love you" or equivalent moment. |
| **Growing Complications** | 50-65% | More obstacles; external pressure; internal fears surface; the couple struggles to maintain connection |
| **The Lie / Betrayal / Misunderstanding** | 65-70% | The central relationship obstacle: a lie is exposed, a betrayal occurs, or a catastrophic misunderstanding |
| **The Breakup** | 70-75% | The couple separates. Both believe it's over. This is the All Is Lost moment for the romance. |
| **Dark Night of the Soul** | 75-80% | Both characters suffer alone. Each realizes what they had and what they lost. Often shown in parallel. |
| **Self-Realization** | 80-85% | Each character (especially the protagonist) realizes their flaw and what they need to change |
| **The Grand Gesture** | 85-92% | The protagonist takes a dramatic, public, and vulnerable action to win back the love interest. Must cost the protagonist something. |
| **Reunion / Declaration** | 92-95% | The couple reunites. The declaration of love. The kiss. |
| **New Beginning** | 95-100% | The couple together, showing how love has changed them both. The "happily ever after" visual. |

### Meet-Cute Variations

| Type | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Collision** | Literally crash into each other | Notting Hill (juice spill) |
| **Mistaken Identity** | One thinks the other is someone else | While You Were Sleeping |
| **Adversarial** | They start as enemies or rivals | You've Got Mail, The Proposal |
| **Rescue** | One saves the other from a bad situation | Pretty Woman |
| **Forced Proximity** | Circumstances force them together | The Holiday, Leap Year |
| **Embarrassment** | One is caught in an embarrassing moment | Bridget Jones's Diary |
| **Interference** | A third party introduces them | Hitch |

### Grand Gesture Requirements

1. **Public:** Demonstrates vulnerability; the protagonist risks humiliation
2. **Personal:** References specific shared experiences or inside knowledge
3. **Costly:** The protagonist sacrifices something significant (job, pride, comfort)
4. **Irrevocable:** Cannot be taken back; shows total commitment
5. **Answers the Theme:** Proves the protagonist has changed in exactly the way needed

## 3.5 Action

### Set-Piece Spacing Formula

For a 120-minute action film:

| Set Piece | Timing | Runtime % | Scale | Purpose |
|-----------|--------|-----------|-------|---------|
| **Opening Action** | 0-10 min | 0-8% | Medium-Large | Establish the hero's abilities and the tone of action; hook the audience |
| **First Challenge** | 25-35 min | 21-29% | Medium | First real confrontation with the antagonist's forces; establishes the threat level |
| **Midpoint Set Piece** | 55-65 min | 46-54% | Large | The biggest action sequence so far; major twist or revelation during or after the action |
| **Escalation Battle** | 80-90 min | 67-75% | Large-Very Large | The heroes take a significant loss; the cost of the conflict is made clear |
| **Final Battle / Climax** | 100-115 min | 83-96% | Maximum | The largest, most intense, most personal action sequence; the climax |

### Action Escalation Rules

1. **Each set piece must be bigger than the last** (in scale, stakes, or emotional weight)
2. **Vary the type of action:** chase, fight, explosion, stealth, escape -- don't repeat
3. **Space set pieces 15-25 minutes apart** to allow character development between them
4. **The quietest scenes should follow the loudest action** (contrast principle)
5. **The final action sequence should be the longest** (typically 15-25 minutes)
6. **Every action scene must advance the plot** -- if you can remove it and the story still works, cut it
7. **The hero should get increasingly damaged** through each set piece (escalating cost)

### Action Scene Internal Structure

```
1. SETUP (30 sec - 1 min): Establish geography, stakes, who's fighting, why
2. INITIAL ADVANTAGE (1-2 min): Hero has early success; seems winnable
3. COMPLICATION (30 sec): Something goes wrong; the plan fails
4. ESCALATION (2-5 min): The fight gets harder; obstacles multiply; improvisation required
5. DARKEST MOMENT (30 sec): Hero is about to lose
6. TURN (15 sec): Hero finds an opening, uses cleverness or newly-learned skill
7. CLIMAX (1-2 min): The decisive blow; the victory (or costly retreat)
8. AFTERMATH (30 sec - 1 min): Consequences; injuries; emotional beat
```

## 3.6 Mystery / Crime

### Red Herring Placement Formula

| Phase | Runtime % | Red Herring Type | Description |
|-------|-----------|-----------------|-------------|
| **Setup** | 0-15% | None | Establish the crime, victim, and initial suspects. No misdirection yet. |
| **Early Investigation** | 15-30% | **Motive Red Herring** | A suspect with obvious motive is introduced; seems too perfect. Multiple suspects given seemingly equal motive. |
| **Midpoint** | 30-50% | **Evidence Red Herring** | Physical evidence or testimony points to the wrong suspect. The detective follows a false trail. |
| **Deepening** | 50-65% | **Behavioral Red Herring** | A character acts suspiciously for reasons unrelated to the crime. Secretive behavior explained by a personal matter, not guilt. |
| **Pre-Climax** | 65-80% | **Last-Minute Red Herring** | One final compelling false lead diverts attention just before the true reveal. The most dramatic misdirection. |
| **Revelation** | 80-90% | **Reveal** | The true culprit is identified. All red herrings are explained. |

### Mystery Revelation Timing

| Element | Runtime % | Description |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| Crime / Inciting Event | 1-10% | The crime occurs or is discovered |
| Initial Suspects (3-5) | 10-20% | The pool of suspects is established |
| First Clue (genuine) | 15-25% | A real clue is planted but its significance is obscured |
| First Red Herring | 20-30% | A false trail begins |
| Suspect Narrowing | 30-40% | One suspect seems most likely (but isn't) |
| Midpoint Twist | 45-55% | A revelation changes the detective's understanding; new suspect or motive emerges |
| Second Genuine Clue | 55-65% | Another real clue; may be connected to the first |
| False Accusation | 60-70% | The detective accuses the wrong person (or the audience firmly believes the wrong suspect) |
| Darkest Moment | 70-80% | The case seems unsolvable; or the detective is personally threatened |
| Lightbulb Moment | 80-85% | The detective connects the clues; sees the pattern; has the epiphany |
| Confrontation / Reveal | 85-92% | The detective confronts the true culprit; the solution is explained |
| Denouement | 92-100% | All loose ends tied up; the detective explains how each clue and red herring fit |

### Fair Play Rules (for Audience-Solvable Mysteries)

1. The audience must have access to the same clues as the detective
2. Every genuine clue must be shown on screen (not just told later)
3. The solution must be logically derivable from the clues presented
4. No information can be withheld that the protagonist has
5. The culprit must be a character the audience has met before the 50% mark
6. The solution should feel "surprising but inevitable" -- when revealed, the audience says "I should have seen it"

## 3.7 Sci-Fi

### World-Building Integration Formula

| Phase | Runtime % | World-Building Allocation | Description |
|-------|-----------|---------------------------|-------------|
| **Opening Image** | 0-2% | HIGH (visual) | Show, don't tell. The first shot establishes the world through environment, technology, or society |
| **Setup** | 2-15% | MEDIUM-HIGH | Introduce world rules through character interaction, not exposition dumps. "Rule of 3": establish no more than 3 major world rules in the first 15 minutes |
| **Adventure Begins** | 15-30% | MEDIUM | World-building is woven into action and character decisions. New elements introduced as needed by the plot |
| **Deep World** | 30-50% | LOW-MEDIUM | The audience now understands the basics. New world elements only introduced when they create conflict or raise stakes |
| **Consequences** | 50-75% | LOW | World rules are tested and consequences are shown. The audience sees how the world's rules create specific dangers |
| **Climax** | 75-95% | MINIMAL (payoff) | No new world rules. The climax must work within already-established rules. The world's rules should be the source of both the problem and the solution |
| **Resolution** | 95-100% | REFLECTION | Show how the world has changed (or how the character's understanding of it has changed) |

### Sci-Fi Exposition Rules

1. **The Iceberg Rule:** Show 10%, imply 90%. The world should feel larger than what's shown.
2. **Character-Driven Exposition:** World rules are revealed because a character needs to know, not because the audience does.
3. **Conflict as Exposition:** The best way to show how the world works is through conflict that depends on its rules.
4. **No "As You Know, Bob":** Characters should never explain things they already know to each other.
5. **Visual Exposition:** Use environment, technology, costumes, and background details to convey world rules.
6. **The Three-Question Limit:** Audiences will accept 3 major "just go with it" premises. More than 3 and you risk losing them.

## 3.8 Animation

### Pacing Differences from Live Action

| Element | Live Action | Animation |
|---------|------------|-----------|
| **Scene Duration** | 1.5-3 minutes average | 1-2 minutes average (faster cuts) |
| **Dialogue Density** | Higher; can sustain talking scenes | Lower; more visual storytelling |
| **Action Sequences** | Constrained by physics and budget | Unconstrained; can be more elaborate and longer |
| **Emotional Beats** | Rely heavily on actor performance | Rely on visual exaggeration, music, and timing |
| **Comedy Timing** | Naturalistic pauses | Compressed timing; quicker beats |
| **World-Building** | Shown through sets and locations | Can be shown through impossible environments |
| **Runtime** | 90-150 min typical | 75-120 min typical (often 85-100) |

### Animation-Specific Structure (Pixar/Disney Model)

| Beat | Runtime % | Duration (90-min film) | Description |
|------|-----------|----------------------|-------------|
| Opening Number/Sequence | 0-5% | 0-4.5 min | Often a musical number or visual sequence that establishes world and tone |
| "I Want" Moment | 8-12% | 7-11 min | The protagonist expresses their desire (often literally in song) |
| Sidekick/Companion Introduction | 15-25% | 13-23 min | The comedic relief and/or emotional companion is introduced |
| Fun & Discovery Montage | 30-45% | 27-40 min | Extended sequences of humor and wonder |
| Emotional Core Scene | 50-55% | 45-50 min | The quiet, emotional scene that reveals the story's heart |
| Darkest Moment + Quiet Song | 70-75% | 63-68 min | Emotional devastation; often accompanied by a ballad or quiet score |
| Rally/Inspiration Moment | 80-85% | 72-77 min | The protagonist is inspired to try again |
| Action Climax | 85-95% | 77-86 min | The biggest action sequence; highly visual |
| Emotional Resolution | 95-100% | 86-90 min | The heart of the story resolves; often tears |

# PART 4: SCENE-LEVEL STRUCTURE

## 4.1 Single Scene Architecture

### The Scene Triad: Goal - Conflict - Outcome

Every scene must contain these three elements:

| Element | Description | Questions to Ask |
|---------|-------------|------------------|
| **Goal** | What does the POV character want in this scene? | What specific, concrete thing is the character trying to achieve RIGHT NOW? |
| **Conflict** | What opposes the character's goal? | Who or what stands in the way? How does opposition manifest? |
| **Outcome** | What is the result? | Did they get what they wanted? (Ideally: No, and things got worse) |

### Four Possible Scene Outcomes

| Outcome | Symbol | Description | Effect on Story |
|---------|--------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Yes** | + | Character achieves goal | Boring if used too often; use sparingly before a major setback |
| **Yes, But...** | +/- | Goal achieved, but at a cost or with a complication | Creates momentum; most common in rising action |
| **No** | - | Character fails | Creates setback; useful for dark moments |
| **No, And Furthermore...** | -/- | Character fails and the situation gets worse | Creates maximum tension; drives the story forward most powerfully |

**Rule:** In scenes throughout the rising action, prefer "No, and furthermore..." or "Yes, but..." outcomes. Save "Yes" for the climax and resolution.

### Scene-Sequel Pattern (Dwight Swain)

A complete dramatic unit consists of a SCENE followed by a SEQUEL:

**SCENE (Action):**
```
1. GOAL:     The character states or reveals what they want
2. CONFLICT: Opposition is encountered; obstacles arise
3. DISASTER: The attempt fails (or succeeds with terrible cost)
```

**SEQUEL (Reaction):**
```
1. REACTION: Emotional response to the disaster (feeling)
2. DILEMMA:  The character considers options (thinking)
3. DECISION: The character chooses a new course of action (deciding)
```

The DECISION of the sequel becomes the GOAL of the next scene, creating a chain:

```
Scene 1 (Goal -> Conflict -> Disaster) ->
  Sequel 1 (Reaction -> Dilemma -> Decision) ->
    Scene 2 (new Goal -> Conflict -> Disaster) ->
      Sequel 2 (Reaction -> Dilemma -> Decision) ->
        Scene 3...
```

### Motivation-Reaction Units (MRUs)

Within each scene, the smallest unit of storytelling is the MRU:

**MOTIVATION (External):** Something happens in the environment that the character perceives.
- Always described objectively (what a camera would see)
- Presented in real time
- Causes a reaction

**REACTION (Internal):** The character's response, presented in this order:
1. **Feeling** (involuntary emotional response -- instantaneous)
2. **Reflex** (involuntary physical response -- immediate)
3. **Rational Action** (voluntary physical response -- deliberate)
4. **Speech** (verbal response -- most deliberate)

Example:
```
MOTIVATION: The door slammed open and a figure stood in the shadows holding a knife.
REACTION:
  1. Fear flooded through her. [feeling]
  2. She stumbled backward, knocking over a chair. [reflex]
  3. She reached for the phone on the counter. [rational action]
  4. "Stay away from me." [speech]
```

## 4.2 Scene Transitions

### Types of Scene Transitions

| Transition | Description | Effect | Best Used For |
|------------|-------------|--------|---------------|
| **Cut** | Instant transition; one shot to the next | Neutral; maintains pace | Standard scene changes |
| **Hard Cut** | Abrupt transition between contrasting scenes | Jarring; creates emphasis | Comedy (ironic juxtaposition); shock |
| **Match Cut** | Visual or thematic similarity between final frame and first frame of next scene | Elegant; creates connection | Showing thematic parallels; time jumps |
| **Smash Cut** | Instant cut from a calm scene to a chaotic one (or vice versa) | Shock; comedy | Wake-up scenes; ironic reveals |
| **J-Cut** | Audio from the next scene begins before the visual transition | Creates anticipation; smooths transition | Dialogue scenes; building tension |
| **L-Cut** | Audio from the current scene continues over the visual of the next scene | Creates continuity; emotional carry-over | Emotional scenes; voiceover |
| **Dissolve** | Current scene fades out as next scene fades in; overlapping | Passage of time; dream-like | Time jumps; memory sequences |
| **Fade to Black** | Scene fades to black before next scene | Finality; passage of significant time | Act breaks; major time jumps |
| **Wipe** | New scene "wipes" across the old scene | Adventurous; serialized feel | Adventure films (Star Wars); comedy |
| **Iris** | Circular wipe (old film technique) | Nostalgic; playful | Animation; period pieces |
| **Jump Cut** | Cut within the same shot; time skips forward | Unease; passage of time; urgency | Montages; character disorientation |
| **Montage** | Series of short scenes compressed | Passage of time; training; development | Training sequences; relationship development |
| **Cross-Cut / Parallel Edit** | Alternating between two simultaneous scenes | Tension; comparison; building toward convergence | Climax sequences; thematic parallels |

### Transition Selection Rules

1. **Most scenes use simple cuts.** Fancy transitions should be rare and meaningful.
2. **Match cuts should connect thematically related ideas** -- not just visual similarities.
3. **Time jumps need clear signals:** dissolves, title cards, or contextual cues.
4. **The transition style should match the genre:** hard cuts for thrillers, dissolves for dramas, wipes for adventure.
5. **Transitions create rhythm:** varying transition types creates pacing variation.

## 4.3 Dialogue Patterns

### Exposition in Dialogue

| Technique | Description | Example Application |
|-----------|-------------|---------------------|
| **The Pope in the Pool** | Deliver exposition during an interesting visual or action | Character explains plan while doing something visually engaging |
| **Conflict-Driven Exposition** | Information emerges through argument | Two characters disagree, and their argument reveals backstory |
| **Fish Out of Water** | A newcomer asks natural questions | New employee; outsider; child asking parents |
| **Gradual Reveal** | Information delivered in pieces across multiple scenes | Each scene adds one new piece of the puzzle |
| **Show, Then Confirm** | Visual storytelling first; dialogue confirms later | Show a character limping; later dialogue reveals old injury |
| **Deadline/Urgency** | Exposition delivered under time pressure | Briefing before a mission; countdown to disaster |

### Subtext Patterns

| Pattern | Surface Meaning | Hidden Meaning | How to Write |
|---------|----------------|----------------|--------------|
| **Displacement** | Characters talk about something trivial | They're really talking about their relationship | Mundane topic echoes emotional truth |
| **Avoidance** | Character talks around a subject | They're afraid to confront the real issue | What they DON'T say is more important |
| **Coded Language** | Characters use seemingly normal phrases | The phrases carry specific shared meaning | Establish the code earlier in the story |
| **Contradiction** | Words say one thing, behavior says another | Internal conflict made external | "I'm fine" (clearly not fine) |
| **The Unsaid** | Conversation has deliberate gaps | Both characters know but won't address | Silence speaks; topic is conspicuously absent |

### Dialogue Conflict Patterns

| Pattern | Description | Escalation |
|---------|-------------|------------|
| **Verbal Sparring** | Characters trade barbs; competitive wit | Low -> Medium |
| **Passive-Aggressive** | Polite surface; hostile undercurrent | Simmering; explodes later |
| **Interrogation** | One character pushes; the other resists | Steady escalation to breaking point |
| **Cross-Purposes** | Characters want different things from the same conversation | Tension builds as goals collide |
| **Ultimatum** | One character forces a decision | Immediate high tension |
| **Confession** | One character reveals a painful truth | Emotional explosion |

## 4.4 Visual Storytelling Beats Within a Scene

### The Visual Story Beat Sequence

```
1. ESTABLISHING CONTEXT (Wide/Master Shot)
   - Where are we? What time is it? Who is present?
   - Duration: 2-5 seconds

2. SPATIAL RELATIONSHIP (Medium Wide)
   - How do characters relate to each other physically?
   - Power dynamics shown through position, height, distance
   - Duration: 3-8 seconds

3. CHARACTER FOCUS (Medium Shot)
   - The scene's initiator begins; dialogue starts
   - Body language conveys intention
   - Duration: varies with dialogue

4. EMOTIONAL REVEAL (Close-Up)
   - Key emotional moments shown in tight framing
   - Reaction shots show impact of dialogue/action
   - Duration: 2-5 seconds per close-up

5. EMPHASIS (Extreme Close-Up or Insert)
   - A hand reaching for a weapon, a tear falling, an object that matters
   - Duration: 1-3 seconds

6. TRANSITION (Movement or Composition Shift)
   - Camera or character movement signals scene shift
   - A character walking away, a door closing, a look exchanged
```

## 4.5 Shot Composition Formulas

### Standard Scene Coverage Pattern

```
1. ESTABLISHING SHOT (Wide/Extreme Wide)
   Purpose: Orient the audience
   Duration: 2-5 seconds
   When: Opening of a new location or time jump

2. MASTER SHOT (Wide)
   Purpose: Show all characters and geography
   Duration: Runs through entire scene (used as backbone)
   When: The "safety" shot; entire scene played in one shot

3. MEDIUM TWO-SHOT
   Purpose: Show relationship between two characters
   Duration: Varies
   When: Conversation; characters interacting

4. OVER-THE-SHOULDER (OTS)
   Purpose: Connect audience to one character's perspective
   Duration: Varies (dialogue exchanges)
   When: Dialogue scenes; creates sense of participation

5. MEDIUM CLOSE-UP
   Purpose: Show character emotion with some environment
   Duration: Dialogue lines; reactions
   When: Important dialogue; character decisions

6. CLOSE-UP
   Purpose: Intimate emotional connection
   Duration: 2-5 seconds per beat
   When: Key emotional moments; reactions; revelations

7. INSERT / EXTREME CLOSE-UP
   Purpose: Draw attention to specific detail
   Duration: 1-3 seconds
   When: Clues; important objects; emotional symbols
```

### Emotional Intensity Through Shot Size

```
LOW INTENSITY -----> HIGH INTENSITY

Extreme Wide -> Wide -> Medium -> Medium Close-Up -> Close-Up -> Extreme Close-Up

As emotional intensity rises, shots get TIGHTER.
As scenes resolve, shots get WIDER.
```

# PART 5: PACING FORMULAS

## 5.1 Tension Curves

### The Sawtooth Pattern (Most Common)

```
Tension
  ^
  |        /\          /\     /\
  |       /  \        /  \   /  \    /\  CLIMAX
  |      /    \      /    \ /    \  /  \
  |     /      \    /      X      \/    \
  |    /        \  /                     \___
  |   /          \/
  |  /
  | /
  +----------------------------------------------> Time
  0%    25%    50%    75%    100%
```

**Rules:**
- Each peak should be higher than the last
- Each valley should be higher than the last (overall upward trend)
- Valleys provide "breathing room" for character development
- The deepest valley comes at 75% (All Is Lost / Dark Night of the Soul)
- The highest peak is the climax (90-95%)

### Tension Curve Variations by Genre

| Genre | Curve Shape | Description |
|-------|------------|-------------|
| **Thriller** | Steep sawtooth; high starting tension | Starts tense, stays tense; brief valleys |
| **Horror** | Long slow build; sharp peaks | Extended quiet periods punctuated by spikes |
| **Comedy** | Regular waves; moderate amplitude | Consistent rhythm; no extended tension |
| **Drama** | Gradual rise; deep valleys | Slower build; emphasis on emotional depth in valleys |
| **Action** | Rapid oscillation; high amplitude | Fast tension-release cycles; brief recovery |
| **Romance** | Gentle rise; emotional peaks | Tension is emotional, not physical; warmth in valleys |
| **Mystery** | Staircase pattern; escalating plateaus | Each revelation raises the baseline tension |

---

## 5.2 Comedy Timing

### Rule of Threes: Detailed Timing

```
Beat 1 (Setup):        1-3 seconds     | Establish pattern
[Pause]:               0.5-1 second    | Let it land
Beat 2 (Reinforcement): 1-3 seconds    | Confirm pattern
[Pause]:               0.5-1 second    | Audience anticipates
Beat 3 (Subversion):   0.5-2 seconds   | BREAK pattern -> LAUGH
[Hold]:                1-2 seconds     | Let the laugh happen
```

### Callback Timing in a 90-Minute Comedy

| Callback | Approximate Time | Minutes Since Last | Effect |
|----------|------------------|--------------------|--------|
| Original Joke | 10-15 min | -- | Normal laugh |
| First Callback | 35-45 min | 20-30 min later | Bigger laugh (recognition) |
| Second Callback | 70-80 min | 30-40 min later | Biggest laugh (anticipation + new context) |
| Tag Callback | 85-90 min | 10-15 min later | Delight (surprise recurrence) |

### Comedy Pacing Rhythm

```
Setup Scene (2-3 min) -> Joke density: LOW (1-2 laughs)
Development (3-5 min) -> Joke density: MEDIUM (3-5 laughs)
Escalation (2-3 min)  -> Joke density: HIGH (5-8 laughs)
Big Set Piece (3-5 min) -> Joke density: VERY HIGH (8-12 laughs)
Emotional Scene (2-3 min) -> Joke density: ZERO (character moment)
Recovery (1-2 min) -> Joke density: LOW (gentle humor returns)
```

**Key Rule:** After every sustained comedy sequence, include a "breathing" scene. Pure emotional content. This makes the next comedy sequence hit harder by contrast.

## 5.3 Emotional Rhythm Patterns

### The Emotional Sine Wave

```
Positive Emotion (Joy, Hope, Love)
  ^
  |    /\              /\
  |   /  \            /  \         /\ RESOLUTION
  |  /    \          /    \       /  \
  +-/------\--------/------\-----/----\---> Time
  |         \      /        \   /
  |          \    /          \ /
  |           \  /            V (Darkest moment)
  |            \/
  v
Negative Emotion (Fear, Sadness, Anger)
```

### Emotional Beat Sequencing

**Rule:** Never sustain the same emotion for more than 3 consecutive scenes. Alternate:

```
Tense Scene -> Humorous Scene -> Emotional Scene -> Tense Scene
OR
Action -> Reflection -> Discovery -> Action
OR
Conflict -> Connection -> Complication -> Conflict
```

### Emotional Exhaustion Prevention

| Scene Count | Emotional Load | Required Follow-Up |
|-------------|---------------|-------------------|
| 1 intense scene | Normal | Continue normally |
| 2 intense scenes in a row | High | Must include 1 lighter scene before next intense scene |
| 3 intense scenes in a row | Exhaustion risk | MUST include 2 lighter scenes; or one significantly lighter scene |
| 4+ intense scenes in a row | Audience disengagement | Avoid at all costs; audience becomes numb |

## 5.4 Scene Length Variation Patterns

### Ideal Scene Length Distribution (Feature Film)

| Scene Length | Percentage of Film | Typical Use |
|-------------|-------------------|-------------|
| **Short (30 sec - 1 min)** | 15-20% of scenes | Quick transitions, montage elements, tension beats |
| **Medium (1-3 min)** | 50-60% of scenes | Standard dialogue, development, most plot advancement |
| **Long (3-5 min)** | 15-25% of scenes | Key confrontations, emotional scenes, set pieces |
| **Very Long (5+ min)** | 5-10% of scenes | Major set pieces, climactic sequences, the "signature scene" |

### Scene Length as Pacing Tool

```
FAST PACING:  Short Short Short Short Medium Short Short  (staccato)
              Used for: montages, action sequences, comedy rapid-fire

NORMAL PACING: Medium Short Medium Medium Long Medium Short (varied)
               Used for: most of the film; standard development

SLOW PACING:  Long Medium Long Very Long Medium Long (sustained)
              Used for: emotional climaxes, contemplative moments, building dread

ACCELERATING: Long Medium Medium Short Short Short (compression)
              Used for: building toward climax; increasing urgency

DECELERATING: Short Short Medium Medium Long (expansion)
              Used for: resolution; coming down from climax; reflection
```

### Act-Specific Scene Length Guidelines

| Act | Average Scene Length | Variation | Reason |
|-----|---------------------|-----------|--------|
| Act I (Setup) | 2-3 minutes | Medium variation | Establishing; letting audience settle in |
| Act II-A (Fun & Games) | 1.5-2.5 minutes | High variation | Exploration; variety keeps engagement |
| Midpoint | 3-5 minutes | Low (one sustained scene) | The pivot; needs weight and focus |
| Act II-B (Closing In) | 1-2 minutes | Very high variation | Tension; shorter scenes create urgency |
| Act III (Finale) | 1-3 minutes, trending shorter | Highest variation | Accelerating toward climax; then expanding for resolution |

# PART 6: CHARACTER ARC TEMPLATES

---

## 6.1 The Positive Change Arc

The protagonist starts with a flawed belief (the "Lie"), encounters truth through conflict, and transforms into a better version of themselves.

### Structure

| Phase | Runtime % | Character State | Key Beat |
|-------|-----------|----------------|----------|
| **Living the Lie** | 0-12% | Character believes the Lie; it shapes their worldview and decisions | The Lie is demonstrated through behavior and choices |
| **Confronted by Truth** | 12-25% | Something challenges the Lie; the character resists | The inciting incident forces contact with the Truth |
| **Resistance** | 25-40% | Character clings to the Lie even as evidence mounts | Each obstacle tests the Lie; character doubles down |
| **Growing Doubt** | 40-50% | The Lie starts to crack; small moments of truth-recognition | Midpoint: a significant moment where the Truth feels possible |
| **Torn Between** | 50-65% | Character oscillates between Lie and Truth; internal conflict peaks | Attempts to have it both ways; compromises that fail |
| **Lie Collapses** | 65-75% | The Lie catastrophically fails; the character hits bottom | All Is Lost: the Lie's failure causes maximum pain |
| **Dark Night** | 75-85% | Character faces the void; must choose: Truth or Lie | Sitting with the pain; processing; understanding |
| **Embracing Truth** | 85-90% | Character consciously chooses the Truth; a fundamental shift | The moment of transformation; may be quiet or dramatic |
| **Proving It** | 90-100% | Character acts on the Truth; demonstrates change through action | The climax tests the new belief under maximum pressure |

### Example Template

```
LIE:    "I don't need anyone. I'm better off alone."
TRUTH:  "Vulnerability and connection make us stronger."
WANT:   To succeed independently (external goal)
NEED:   To let people in (internal need)

ACT I:   Character succeeds alone but is isolated and unhappy
MIDPOINT: Character connects with someone; glimpses what they're missing
CRISIS:  Going it alone causes catastrophic failure
CLIMAX:  Character asks for help; vulnerability leads to victory
```

---

## 6.2 The Negative Change Arc (Disillusionment / Fall)

The protagonist starts with either a truth or a lie, and through the story's events, descends into a darker state. They end worse than they began.

### Variant A: Disillusionment Arc

Character starts believing a positive Lie and discovers a devastating Truth.

| Phase | Runtime % | Character State | Key Beat |
|-------|-----------|----------------|----------|
| **Naive Belief** | 0-12% | Character believes the world is a certain (positive) way | Innocence and optimism on display |
| **First Cracks** | 12-25% | Evidence that the world isn't what they thought | Discrepancies that can still be explained away |
| **Holding On** | 25-50% | Character fights to maintain their belief despite mounting evidence | Increasing cognitive dissonance |
| **Midpoint Shattering** | 50% | An undeniable revelation destroys the positive belief | The Lie is unmistakably exposed |
| **Bitter Transformation** | 50-75% | Character becomes cynical, angry, or numb | Rejection of former ideals |
| **New (Darker) Normal** | 75-100% | Character arrives at a harder, colder truth | Survival at the cost of innocence |

### Variant B: Fall Arc (Corruption / Tragedy)

Character rejects the Truth in favor of the Lie, leading to their destruction.

| Phase | Runtime % | Character State | Key Beat |
|-------|-----------|----------------|----------|
| **Normal Person** | 0-12% | Character has relatable goals and sympathetic qualities | Audience identifies with them |
| **Temptation** | 12-25% | An opportunity to take a dark shortcut presents itself | The first moral compromise |
| **Rationalization** | 25-40% | Character justifies the dark choice; it seems to work | Success through corruption |
| **Escalation** | 40-55% | Each subsequent choice is darker; the character is in deeper | Point of no return passed |
| **Midpoint: Full Commitment** | 50-55% | Character fully embraces the dark path | "I am the one who knocks" moment |
| **Consequences Begin** | 55-70% | The dark choices start harming those the character loves | The cost of corruption made visible |
| **Denial** | 70-80% | Character refuses to acknowledge they've become the villain | Loss of self-awareness |
| **Recognition (Too Late)** | 80-90% | Character sees what they've become but cannot undo it | Tragic recognition |
| **Destruction** | 90-100% | The character is destroyed by their own choices | Tragic ending; the Lie consumes them |

## 6.3 The Flat Arc (The Steadfast Character)

The character does NOT change. Instead, they change the WORLD (or other characters) around them through their unwavering commitment to the Truth.

### Structure

| Phase | Runtime % | Character State | World State |
|-------|-----------|----------------|-------------|
| **Truth Established** | 0-12% | Character already knows the Truth; it defines them | The world is broken, wrong, or oppressive |
| **World Challenges Truth** | 12-25% | The world pushes back against the character's Truth | Characters around them believe the Lie |
| **Testing Begins** | 25-40% | Increasing pressure to abandon the Truth | Other characters argue for the Lie |
| **First Convert** | 40-50% | The character's steadfastness inspires one person to change | An ally sees the Truth |
| **Maximum Pressure** | 50-70% | The world brings its greatest force against the character | The character is tested to their absolute limit |
| **Darkest Moment** | 70-80% | It seems the Truth will lose; the Lie will win | The character's faith is tested but holds |
| **Truth Prevails** | 80-95% | The character's unwavering belief inspires widespread change | The world transforms around them |
| **New World** | 95-100% | The character is unchanged, but the world is reborn | The Truth has replaced the Lie in the world |

### Example Template

```
TRUTH: "Every person has inherent worth."
WORLD'S LIE: "Some people are disposable."

The character never wavers from this belief.
Through their example, they change every character around them.
At the climax, the world itself is transformed by the Truth.
The character at the end is the same person -- but the world is different.
```

## 6.4 Ensemble Cast Dynamics

### Ensemble Character Distribution

| Role | Function | Arc Type | Screen Time |
|------|----------|----------|-------------|
| **The Protagonist** | Drives the A-Plot; embodies the theme | Positive, Negative, or Flat | 25-35% |
| **The Heart** | Emotional center; most relatable | Usually Positive | 10-15% |
| **The Brain** | Plans and strategizes; provides exposition | Often Flat | 10-15% |
| **The Muscle** | Acts decisively; physical capability | Positive or Flat | 8-12% |
| **The Wildcard** | Unpredictable; creates both problems and solutions | Any (often the most interesting arc) | 8-12% |
| **The Skeptic** | Challenges the group; voice of doubt | Positive (convinced over time) | 8-12% |
| **The Outsider** | New to the group; asks audience questions | Positive (grows into the group) | 8-12% |

### Ensemble Arc Interweaving

**Rule:** In an ensemble, character arcs should peak at different times:

```
Character A: ___/\___________/\____/\____________________/\
Character B: _________/\_______________/\__________/\______
Character C: ______________/\__/\_______________/\_________
Character D: _____/\____________________/\__________/\_____

Each character should have their "spotlight episode/sequence" at a different time.
No two characters should hit their lowest point at the same time.
```

### Ensemble Conflict Dynamics

| Dynamic | Description | Function |
|---------|-------------|----------|
| **The Foil Pair** | Two characters who are opposites | Highlight each other's qualities through contrast |
| **The Mentor-Student** | One character teaches another | Creates generational depth and knowledge transfer |
| **The Rivalry** | Two characters competing for the same goal | Creates internal tension that mirrors external conflict |
| **The Unspoken Bond** | Two characters connected by shared trauma | Creates emotional depth without explicit exposition |
| **The Betrayal Setup** | One character who will eventually betray the group | Creates suspense and heartbreak |

## 6.5 Antagonist Arc Formulas

### The Mirror Antagonist

The antagonist represents what the protagonist could become (or could have been):

| Phase | Protagonist | Antagonist | Mirror Effect |
|-------|-------------|------------|---------------|
| **Introduction** | Has the Lie | Has embraced the Lie fully | Audience sees the danger of the protagonist's path |
| **Rising Action** | Struggles with the Lie | Is empowered by the Lie | Success through darkness vs. struggle through growth |
| **Midpoint** | Glimpses the Truth | Doubles down on the Lie | Divergent paths become clear |
| **Climax** | Embraces the Truth | Refuses the Truth | The two paths collide |
| **Resolution** | Transformed by Truth | Destroyed by the Lie | The protagonist sees what they avoided becoming |

### Antagonist Arc Types

| Type | Description | Arc |
|------|-------------|-----|
| **The Force of Nature** | Unstoppable; no arc | Static; represents the challenge itself (shark in Jaws) |
| **The Corrupt Mirror** | Was once like the protagonist | Revealed backstory shows parallel path; may be redeemed |
| **The Sympathetic Villain** | Has understandable motives | Own positive arc that tragically leads to evil methods |
| **The Escalating Threat** | Gets more dangerous over time | Powers or influence grow throughout the story |
| **The Hidden Villain** | True identity concealed | Revelation of identity is a major plot point |
| **The Tragic Antagonist** | Was once good; fell | Negative arc visible in flashbacks or exposition |
| **The System** | Institution or society | Impersonal; protagonist fights the structure itself |

### Antagonist Introduction Timing

| Strategy | When | Effect |
|----------|------|--------|
| **Immediate** | Scene 1 | Establishes threat from the start (Thanos, Darth Vader) |
| **Shadow** | Act I (referenced but unseen) | Builds dread through reputation (Keyser Soze) |
| **Midpoint Reveal** | ~50% | Reframes everything that came before (Usual Suspects) |
| **Late Reveal** | 75%+ | Maximum shock when a "friend" is revealed as the enemy |
| **Parallel Introduction** | Throughout | Antagonist story runs alongside protagonist's (Heat) |
